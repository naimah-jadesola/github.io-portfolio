1. Introduction¶

This project analyzes the DC Public Employee Salary dataset from DC Open Data.This dataset includes employee information such as job title, department, grade, compensation rate, hire date, and appointment type. I picked this dataset because I wanted to look at how pay differs across departments and job positions in DC government, as well as learning about new departments I can use my study in. This was a school project but also an educational personal research project for me.

2. Objective and Research Questions¶

The goal of this project is to explore salary patterns in the DC government data. I also want to use this to learn more about what kind of positions or departments might be good for my own career path.

Research Questions:¶

  1. Which departments and job titles have the highest average pay?
  2. How does pay vary by grade and appointment type?
  3. What are the pay differences between the departments I am most interested in working for?

6. Data Cleaning¶

After looking at the data, I made a few changes to clean it up. I standardized the column names to be easier to work with, filled missing values in the GRADE column with "Unknown" so I wouldn't lose those rows of data, and converted the HIREDATESTRING column into a proper datetime format. I used .str.strip() on the column names because I noticed some had extra spaces, which was causing errors when I tried to select them. I also checked for duplicates, but since none were found, I didn't need to drop any rows.¶

9. Insights and Conclusion¶

This project examined salary patterns in the DC Public Employee Salary dataset by focusing on departments, job titles, grades, appointment types, and hiring trends. The analysis helped answer the research questions by showing where compensation differences appeared most clearly across employee groups.

Several patterns stood out in the visualizations. Compensation was not evenly distributed, and some departments and job titles had noticeably higher pay than others. Grade level and appointment type also appeared to be related to salary differences, while the box plot showed that some employee groups included clear outliers with much higher compensation.

One limitation of this dataset is that it does not explain every reason behind salary differences. Important factors such as education, years of experience, overtime, and department-specific pay structures may not be fully captured. Future analysis could look more closely at how length of service or department-level trends relate to employee compensation.
